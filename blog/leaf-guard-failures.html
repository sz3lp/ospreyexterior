Gutter science

Why leaf guards fail in the Pacific Northwest

Fir needles, alder catkins, and moss overwhelm most guard systems. Understand the weaknesses so you can choose upgrades that actually protect your home.

We service thousands of linear feet of gutter guard systems across Bellevue, Kirkland, and Seattle. Here’s what consistently goes wrong—and what to do about it.

Micro-mesh guards clog from pollen mats

Fine screens trap pollen, sap, and roof granules. Without quarterly rinsing, water sheets over the top. We recommend seasonal cleaning and occasional removal to clear debris from the gutter trough.

Reverse-curve guards overflow during downpours

Reverse-curve designs rely on surface tension. During intense rain, water misses the lip entirely. These systems also let needles enter at the seam, causing backups at the first elbow.

Foam inserts become compost

Foam absorbs water, captures seeds, and quickly disintegrates. We frequently remove collapsing foam and replace it with open gutters plus scheduled cleanings.

Upgrade path that works

Pair open gutters with documented cleaning cycles, or install hinged perforated covers that can be lifted during maintenance. Our gutter installation team designs systems that balance protection and serviceability.

Guard type Failure mode Maintenance interval
Micro-mesh Pollen mats block water Every 3 months
Reverse-curve Overflow during heavy rain Every 6 months
Foam insert Decomposes and clogs outlets Replace annually
